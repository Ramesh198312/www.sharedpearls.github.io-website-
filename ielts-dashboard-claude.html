IELTS Listening · 30 Minutes · 40 Questions · Sections 1–4

Listening Practice

The recording is played once only. Sections increase in difficulty. British, Australian, and American accents are all used. You have 10 minutes after the test to transfer your answers to the answer sheet.

The Four Sections
Section 01 · Questions 1–10
Everyday Social Dialogue
A conversation between two speakers in an everyday social context — booking a holiday, registering at a library, or arranging a gym membership. Form, table, and note completion. Easiest section.
Section 02 · Questions 11–20
Monologue: Social or Work Context
One speaker presenting information of general interest — a tour guide explaining a local attraction, or a manager presenting workplace procedures. Map or diagram labelling common here.
Section 03 · Questions 21–30
Academic or Training Discussion
Up to four speakers in an academic or training context — students discussing an assignment with a tutor, or a seminar. Multiple choice and matching questions. Contains deliberate distractors.
Section 04 · Questions 31–40
Academic Lecture or Monologue
A university-level lecture or academic talk. One speaker throughout. The most challenging section — requires understanding of complex argument and specialist vocabulary. Note and summary completion.

Band 7+ Strategies
Examiner-Approved Technique
  • Read ahead — use every pause between sections to preview upcoming questions and underline key words.
  • Distractor awareness — speakers often mention something and then correct or reject it. Wait for the final answer.
  • Spelling matters — a correct answer spelled incorrectly is marked wrong. Practise: accommodation, guarantee, necessary.
  • Word limit is absolute — three words when the instruction says two is an automatic zero.
  • Answers are in order — in most question types the answers follow the sequence of the recording.
  • Transfer carefully — you have 10 minutes after the recording ends to write answers on the answer sheet. Check every word.

Band 7+ Reading Strategies
Examiner-Approved Technique
  • Skim first — spend 2–3 minutes reading each passage for gist before looking at any questions.
  • True vs Not Given — "Not Given" means the idea is absent from the text entirely. Never assume.
  • Headings — focus on topic sentences of each paragraph (first or last line), not supporting detail.
  • Time discipline — 20 minutes per passage maximum. If stuck on a question, mark and move on.
  • Paraphrasing — questions almost never use the exact words of the text. Train yourself to spot synonyms.
  • No transfer time — unlike Listening, you must write Reading answers directly on the answer sheet during the 60 minutes.

📊 Task 1 Question — Bar Chart
The bar chart below shows the percentage of households in four different income groups who owned at least one car in the United Kingdom in 1970, 1990, and 2010.

Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant. Write at least 150 words.
✦ Model Answer · Band 8.0
Band 8.0

The bar chart illustrates car ownership rates across four household income groups in the UK at three time points: 1970, 1990, and 2010. Overall, car ownership increased across all income groups over the period, with the most marked growth recorded among lower-income households.

In 1970, car ownership was strongly correlated with income. High-income households reported the highest ownership rate at approximately 78%, compared with just 14% for the lowest-income group. By 1990, this gap had narrowed considerably, with high-income ownership reaching 92% while the lowest-income figure rose to 31%.

The most striking development over the forty-year period was the tripling of car ownership in the lowest-income bracket, which climbed from 14% to 44% by 2010. In contrast, high-income ownership grew more modestly, plateauing at around 96% and suggesting near-saturation in that group by 2010.

Middle-income households followed a broadly similar upward trajectory, with both groups approximately doubling their 1970 figures to reach 61% and 81% respectively by the end of the period.

Task 1 Examiner Checklist
  • Paraphrase the question in your introduction — never copy it.
  • Write an overview as your second paragraph — the single most important step for Band 7+.
  • Include specific data — percentages, figures, and values to support descriptions.
  • Use comparative language: in contrast · whereas · while · compared with · by contrast.
  • Do NOT include a personal opinion or conclusion. Task 1 is purely objective description.
  • Minimum 150 words. Optimal length: 170–190 words.
✍ Task 2 Question — Discussion Essay
Some people believe that the widespread use of artificial intelligence will have an overwhelmingly negative impact on society. Others argue that it presents opportunities that far outweigh any potential risks.

Discuss both views and give your own opinion. Give reasons for your answer and include any relevant examples from your own knowledge or experience. Write at least 250 words.
✦ Model Answer · Band 8.5
Band 8.5

The rapid proliferation of artificial intelligence has prompted considerable debate about its long-term societal consequences. While critics rightly point to the risks of technological unemployment and erosion of human agency, I would argue that the transformative potential of AI substantially outweighs its dangers — provided adequate governance frameworks are established.

Those who view AI negatively frequently cite its demonstrated capacity to displace workers across a wide range of industries. Research from the Oxford Martin School suggested that up to 47% of American jobs faced a high risk of automation within two decades. Furthermore, the concentration of AI-generated wealth among a small technological elite risks exacerbating existing socioeconomic inequalities in ways that current redistributive mechanisms are ill-equipped to address.

Nevertheless, these concerns — while legitimate — overlook the considerable benefits AI has already delivered in fields such as medicine, climate science, and education. AI diagnostic tools have demonstrated the ability to detect certain cancers more accurately than experienced clinicians, with potentially life-saving implications for healthcare systems under resource pressure globally.

In conclusion, the central question is not whether AI will transform society — it demonstrably will — but whether that transformation is governed equitably. With appropriate international regulation and redistributive policy, AI represents one of the most significant opportunities for human progress in the modern era.

Task 2 Examiner Checklist
  • Introduction — paraphrase topic + state your position clearly.
  • Body Para 1 — first view: argument + evidence + example.
  • Body Para 2 — second view or your own position: argument + evidence.
  • Conclusion — restate your position. Never introduce new ideas here.
  • Use a range of cohesive devices — not only "however" and "furthermore".
  • Target 270–290 words. Never write fewer than 250.
  • Avoid informal language: "lots of," "kids," "things," "also" at sentence start.
  • Replace "I think" with: "It can be argued that…" / "The evidence suggests…"
IELTS Speaking · 11–14 Minutes · Face-to-Face with a Certified Examiner

Speaking Practice

The test is recorded. You are assessed on Fluency & Coherence · Lexical Resource · Grammatical Range & Accuracy · Pronunciation. Accent does not affect your score — clarity and range do.

Three Parts
Part 01 · 4–5 Minutes
Introduction & Interview
Familiar topics: home, work, studies, hobbies, everyday life. Short answers expected — extend with reason or example.
Part 02 · 3–4 Minutes
Individual Long Turn — Cue Card
1 minute preparation + 1–2 minutes speaking on a given topic. Examiner may ask 1–2 follow-up questions.
Part 03 · 4–5 Minutes
Two-Way Discussion
Abstract discussion connected to Part 2 topic. Longer, more complex answers expected. Demonstrate reasoning and opinion.
Sample Part 1 Questions
Topic: Education & Learning
  • What subjects did you enjoy most at school, and why?
  • Do you think the education system in your country prepares young people well for the world of work?
  • Have you ever taken any online courses? What was that experience like?
Topic: Technology & Daily Life
  • How much time do you spend on your phone each day? Do you think this is too much?
  • Are there any apps you find particularly useful in your daily life?
  • Do you prefer to communicate with friends online or face to face? Why?
Part 2 — Cue Card Task
Describe a book you have read that had a significant impact on you.
  • What the book was and who wrote it
  • When you read it and why you chose it
  • What the book was about
  • And explain why it had such a significant impact on you
You have 1 minute to prepare. You should then speak for 1 to 2 minutes. The examiner will ask you one or two questions at the end.
Sample Part 3 Questions — Abstract Discussion
Connected Topic: Books, Reading & Knowledge
  • Do you think people read fewer books now than they did in the past? What might explain this trend?
  • What are the advantages of reading physical books compared with e-books or audiobooks?
  • Some argue that fiction has no practical value in modern education. To what extent do you agree?
  • How important is it for schools to encourage children to read for pleasure, rather than exclusively for academic purposes?
Assessment Criteria — What the Examiner Is Evaluating
Fluency & Coherence
Speaks at length without inappropriate pauses. Ideas are logically connected. Uses discourse markers naturally and accurately.
Lexical Resource
Uses a wide vocabulary range appropriately. Can paraphrase effectively when an exact word is unavailable. Avoids repetition.
Grammatical Range
Produces a variety of complex structures spontaneously. Minor errors that do not impede communication are acceptable at Band 7.
Pronunciation
Speech is intelligible throughout. Features of connected speech are present. No single accent is penalised — clarity is what counts.
💡 High-Score Speaking Strategies
Extend every Part 1 answer — give a reason, example, or contrast. A single-sentence answer signals Band 5.
Use hedging naturally — "I suppose…", "It seems to me that…", "To a certain extent…", "I'd like to think that…"
Self-correct your errors — examiners reward self-repair. "I was — sorry, I have been teaching for three years."
Vary your vocabulary — replace "good" with: beneficial, rewarding, advantageous, fruitful, enriching, invaluable.
In Part 3, argue two sides — "On one hand… however, on the other…" This demonstrates reasoning depth.
Use your preparation minute in Part 2 — write 3–4 bullet points. Structure: intro → main experience → reason → reflection.
Pronunciation over accent — speak clearly at a natural pace. Do not slow down artificially — it reduces your fluency score.

From the Examiner's Desk · Cambridge Standard

Insider Examiner Tips

Guidance drawn from official IELTS band descriptors, Cambridge assessment rubrics, and the most common errors systematically identified by trained examiners. These insights separate Band 6 from Band 7.5+.

🎧 Listening · Critical Rules Sections 3 & 4 Focus
⚠The Distractor Trap
In Section 3, speakers frequently mention an answer, then reject or correct it. The examiner deliberately places an attractive distractor before the correct answer. Never write until you are certain the speaker has finished changing their mind.
✦Spelling Always Counts
A correct answer spelled incorrectly is marked wrong — no partial credit. Practise these commonly misspelled IELTS words: accommodation · guarantee · necessary · questionnaire · parliament · colleague · necessary.
✦Word Limit Is Absolute
If instructions say "no more than two words," a three-word answer receives zero marks even if factually correct. Read the instruction on every section before the recording begins.
📖 Reading · Critical Rules True / False / NG Focus
⚠False ≠ Not Given — The Most Costly Confusion
False: the text directly and explicitly contradicts the statement. Not Given: the idea is simply not discussed anywhere in the text. If the passage does not mention the concept at all — even implicitly — the answer is NG, never False. When in doubt: ask yourself, "Does the text address this idea at all?"
✦Skim the Passage Before Reading Questions
Spend 2–3 minutes skimming each passage for general understanding and paragraph structure before reading the questions. Candidates who go straight to questions waste significant time scanning back and forth.
✦Headings Require Main Idea — Not Phrase Matching
The headings task requires identifying the main purpose of each paragraph. Candidates who find a matching phrase and stop reading frequently select a distractor that reflects detail rather than the paragraph's central argument.
✍ Writing · Critical Rules Task 1 & 2 · Band 7+
⚠The Missing Overview Costs You Two Bands
Candidates who omit the overview paragraph in Task 1 cannot achieve above Band 5 for Task Achievement — regardless of how well they describe the data. The overview is not optional: it is the single most important sentence in Task 1.
✗ Band 5:"Nowadays many people think technology is good and bad for society."
✓ Band 7.5+:"The relationship between technological development and societal wellbeing is a source of considerable debate among economists and policy-makers."
✦Replace "I think" with Academic Hedging
Use: "It can be argued that…" / "The evidence suggests…" / "There are compelling reasons to believe that…" / "One might contend that…" These constructions signal academic register and directly improve your Lexical Resource band score.
🎤 Speaking · Critical Rules Parts 2 & 3 Focus
⚠Never Memorise Scripts — It Is Penalised
IELTS examiners are trained to identify and immediately penalise memorised responses. If you deliver a prepared speech, the examiner will change the topic and your Fluency & Coherence band will fall. Practise response frameworks and vocabulary — not word-for-word scripts.
✗ Band 5:"It was very good and I liked it very much because it was interesting."
✓ Band 7.5+:"It was genuinely enriching — it completely shifted my perspective on how I approach problem-solving in professional contexts."
✦Use All 60 Seconds of Part 2 Preparation
During your 1-minute preparation time, write 3–4 structured bullet points on the notepad provided. Follow this framework: (1) Introduction — what/who/where. (2) Main experience or example. (3) Reason for significance. (4) Reflection or broader implication. This prevents running out of content at 45 seconds.
